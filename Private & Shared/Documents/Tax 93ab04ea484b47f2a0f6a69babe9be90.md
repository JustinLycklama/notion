## T5008

Any T5008 With a 20 â€˜Cost or Book Valueâ€™ and a 21 â€˜Proceeds of Dispositionâ€ will be a capital gains type tax

A T5008 slip would not represent capital gains in a few specific situations:

1. **Return of capital**: When a distribution represents a return of your original investment rather than a profit. This reduces your adjusted cost base but isn't immediately taxable.
2. **Security transfers**: If you're simply moving securities between accounts you own (like from one brokerage to another) without actually selling them.
3. **Errors or corrections**: Sometimes T5008s are issued to correct previous information, and might not represent an actual transaction.
4. **Certain derivatives or futures contracts**: Some specialized financial instruments might generate income that's treated differently than capital gains.
5. **Foreign exchange transactions**: Currency exchange transactions might appear on a T5008 but could be treated differently depending on their purpose.
6. **Bond transactions where interest is reported separately**: The interest component would be interest income, not capital gains.

However, the vast majority of T5008 slips with both "Cost or Book value" and "Proceeds of disposition" do represent potential capital gains or losses transactions. If you're seeing numerous T5008 entries for normal stock or ETF transactions, they are almost certainly capital gains/losses.

</aside>
